## MDA Profile Keycaps Overview

### Introduction

Keycap profiles define the shape, height, and sculpting of keycaps on a mechanical keyboard, directly influencing typing comfort, acoustics, and aesthetics. Profiles range from tall and dramatically sculpted (SA) to low and uniform (DSA, XDA). MDA occupies a deliberate middle ground — a medium-height sculpted profile with spherical tops that aims to combine the best qualities of both ends of the spectrum.

MDA was created by MelGeek and has since been adopted by multiple manufacturers including Akko, Epomaker, and Keychron. It is often described as a shortened SA — preserving the spherical dish and sculpted rows of SA while reducing the height to something more practical for daily use.

### What is MDA Profile?

MDA is a medium-height sculpted keycap profile with a spherical top surface. It sits between the tall SA profile and the low, uniform DSA profile in both height and sculpting intensity, offering a balanced typing experience that avoids the extremes of either.

Typical characteristics:

- **Overall height:** Approximately 7.45 mm — shorter than OEM (~11.9 mm) and SA (~16.5 mm), similar to DSA (~7.5 mm)
- **Top surface:** Spherical (concave in both axes, conforming to the fingertip)
- **Sculpting:** Gently sculpted across rows, with each row at a slightly different height and angle
- **Wall thickness:** Moderate
- **Material:** Primarily PBT, with dye-sublimation or doubleshot legends
- **Stem:** Standard MX-style (compatible with any MX switch)

### Design Characteristics

**Row sculpting.** MDA features gentle, organic sculpting across all rows. Each row has a slightly different height and tilt, following the natural curve of finger flexion. The sculpting is less dramatic than on Cherry or OEM — the row-to-row transitions are subtle rather than pronounced. This gives MDA a softer, more gradual feel under the fingers while still providing enough differentiation for touch typists to orient themselves by feel.

**Spherical top surface.** Like SA and DSA, MDA uses a spherical dish rather than the cylindrical shape found on Cherry and OEM. The concave surface cups the fingertip from all directions, naturally centering it on each key. This is particularly useful for typists who strike keys with their finger pads rather than fingertips, as the wider contact area benefits from the spherical guidance.

**Ergonomics.** The medium height keeps hands at a moderate elevation — low enough that a wrist rest is optional rather than mandatory, but tall enough to provide a satisfying sense of key depth. The gentle sculpting reduces strain over long sessions without the abrupt angle changes of more aggressively sculpted profiles.

**Sound.** MDA is one of the quieter keycap profiles. The medium height produces less acoustic resonance than taller profiles like SA or KSA, resulting in a lower-pitched, more subdued sound. The spherical top also distributes keystroke impact differently than cylindrical profiles, contributing to a softer overall acoustic signature.

### Advantages

- **Balanced height.** MDA avoids the extremes — not so tall that you need a wrist rest, not so low that you lose the sense of sculpted key depth. It is a practical middle ground for users who find SA too tall and DSA too flat.
- **Spherical comfort.** The dished tops conform to fingertips naturally, reducing fatigue and missed keystrokes during long typing sessions.
- **Gentle sculpting.** The subtle row differentiation provides orientation without the aggressive angle changes that can cause finger strain on more dramatically sculpted profiles.
- **Quiet acoustics.** Users working in shared or noise-sensitive environments benefit from MDA's subdued sound signature.
- **Good material quality.** Most MDA sets are produced in PBT with dye-sub or doubleshot legends, offering strong durability and resistance to shine.
- **Growing availability.** Multiple manufacturers now produce MDA sets, expanding the range of colorways and price points available.

### Disadvantages

- **Less tactile row differentiation.** The gentle sculpting means rows feel more similar to each other than on Cherry or OEM. Touch typists who depend on strong sculpt differences for orientation may find MDA less helpful.
- **Limited layout coverage.** MDA sets often lack support for ISO, international, and non-standard layouts. Users with Nordic, UK, or other non-ANSI layouts may struggle to find complete kits.
- **Smaller aftermarket than Cherry or OEM.** While growing, the selection of MDA sets is still modest compared to the hundreds of options available in Cherry profile.
- **Not as distinctive as SA.** Users drawn to the tall, retro aesthetic of SA may find MDA's restrained height visually underwhelming. MDA trades visual drama for practicality.
- **Uniform-adjacent feel.** The subtle sculpting can feel almost flat to users coming from strongly sculpted profiles, making the transition less satisfying than expected.

### Comparison with Other Profiles

| Feature | MDA | Cherry | SA | DSA | XDA |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Height** | Medium (~7.45 mm) | Medium (~9.4 mm) | Tall (~16.5 mm) | Low (~7.5 mm) | Low (~7–8 mm) |
| **Sculpting** | Gentle (stepped rows) | Moderate (4 rows) | Pronounced (multiple rows) | Uniform (no sculpting) | Uniform (no sculpting) |
| **Top surface** | Spherical | Cylindrical | Spherical | Spherical | Flat with slight dish |
| **Material** | PBT (typically) | PBT or ABS | ABS (typically) | PBT or ABS | PBT or ABS |
| **Sound** | Quiet, subdued | Deep, solid | Deep, resonant | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Wrist rest needed** | Optional | Optional | Yes | No | No |
| **Layout coverage** | Limited (mostly ANSI) | Very wide | Wide | Wide | Wide |
| **Best for** | Balanced comfort, quiet typing | Enthusiast daily drivers | Retro aesthetics, typists | Layout flexibility | Wide-key preference |

**MDA vs. Cherry:** Cherry is taller with cylindrical tops and more pronounced sculpting. Cherry offers a wider aftermarket and deeper acoustics; MDA offers a quieter profile with spherical comfort. Choose Cherry for sound and customization variety; choose MDA for a softer, quieter typing experience.

**MDA vs. SA:** MDA is essentially a compressed SA. Both share spherical tops and sculpted rows, but SA is more than twice the height with far more dramatic sculpting. SA delivers the retro aesthetic and deep thock; MDA delivers the spherical comfort in a more practical, daily-driver package.

**MDA vs. DSA:** Both sit at similar heights, but DSA is completely uniform — every row is identical. MDA adds gentle sculpting for ergonomic guidance while maintaining a comparable low profile. Choose DSA for maximum layout flexibility (keycaps are interchangeable between rows); choose MDA for the added comfort of row differentiation.

**MDA vs. XDA:** XDA is uniform with a wider, flatter top surface. MDA is sculpted with a deeper spherical dish. XDA provides a broader finger landing zone; MDA provides better finger centering. Both are relatively quiet profiles.

### Common Use Cases

**Extended typing and writing.** The gentle sculpting and spherical tops reduce finger fatigue during multi-hour typing sessions. The quiet acoustics are a bonus in office and library environments.

**Office and shared environments.** MDA's subdued sound profile makes it one of the more workplace-friendly options among sculpted keycaps — less audible to nearby coworkers than Cherry or SA.

**Users transitioning from SA.** Typists who enjoy the SA spherical feel but want a lower, more practical height will find MDA a natural step down without losing the dished key tops they prefer.

**General-purpose daily driving.** MDA's balanced design makes it a versatile choice that handles typing, light gaming, and everyday computer use without excelling or underperforming in any single area.

**Linear switch pairings.** Community feedback consistently notes that MDA pairs particularly well with linear switches, where the quiet profile and smooth tops complement the uninterrupted keystroke feel.

### Summary

MDA is a balanced, middle-ground keycap profile that combines the spherical comfort of SA with a practical medium height. Choose MDA when you want sculpted ergonomics without the towering height of SA, a quieter sound signature than most sculpted profiles, and a spherical dish that naturally cradles your fingertips. The main trade-offs are limited layout coverage beyond ANSI, a smaller aftermarket selection compared to Cherry, and gentler sculpting that may feel too subtle for users accustomed to aggressively contoured profiles. For typists seeking a profile that does everything reasonably well without pushing to any extreme, MDA is a strong and increasingly available choice.
