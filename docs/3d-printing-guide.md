# 3D Printing Guide

This guide is for makers who want to use the repository files as references for personal, educational, or compatible-accessory printing projects.

## What To Print First

Good first projects include:
- Small accessories that reference an existing case or mounting pattern
- Plate fit-check tools or alignment jigs
- Simple case experiments before attempting a full enclosure

Printing full keyboard or mouse shells may require more iteration, support tuning, and post-processing than accessory parts.

## Start From Reference Geometry

Before slicing anything:
- Open the STEP or drawing files and confirm the part you want to reference
- Re-check critical measurements such as screw spacing, wall thickness, and cutout size
- Confirm units are millimeters after importing into your CAD or slicer workflow

If a plate or drawing file is available, compare it against the 3D model before printing.

## Recommended Workflow

1. Inspect the original file in CAD software.
2. Export only the part you actually want to prototype.
3. Print a small fit-check version when possible.
4. Test screw holes, clearances, and mating surfaces before a full print.
5. Revise your model based on the test fit.

## Material Notes

- PLA is useful for quick prototypes and fit checks.
- PETG is often a better choice when you want more heat resistance and toughness.
- ABS or ASA may be better for more durable enclosure experiments, but they need more controlled printing conditions.
- Flexible or specialty materials can help for feet, dampening parts, or protective accessories.

## Design Checks Before Printing

Look closely at:
- Wall thickness and overhangs
- Screw bosses and threaded areas
- Switch cutouts and stabilizer clearances
- USB port openings and cable clearance
- Tolerances between top and bottom case parts

Do not assume a CAD export is immediately print-ready without checking these areas.
